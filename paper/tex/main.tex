\title{Structural Entropy Dark Energy: a fixed-parameter, $\Lambda$-free\\ dark-energy model from horizon thermodynamics}
\author{Stilian Pandev}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}
\email{stilianpandev@gmail.com}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We present Structural Entropy Dark Energy (SEDE), a $\Lambda$-free model in which dark energy is the
thermodynamic conjugate of the cosmic apparent-horizon entropy, $\rhoDE=\TAH\,\sgrav\,\fsat(z)$, gated
by a structure-growth factor $\fsat$ (normalised to $1$ today) whose redshift dependence follows a
halo-binding-entropy prescription. A single Friedmann fixed-point equation replaces the cosmological
constant by a structure-gated, horizon-coupled term that adds \emph{no fitted dark-energy parameter}
relative to $\LCDM$: the $H$-coupling $\lambda=1/2$ follows from a volume-law horizon entropy (the
maximal Barrow deformation $\Delta=1$, retained only as a falsifiable test axis), the equation of state
from spatial flatness, the coupling $\gamma\approx1.5$ from halo binding, and the sound speed
$c_s^2=1$ from a background functional. In a marginalised, CAMB-in-the-loop joint analysis (DESI DR2
BAO, Pantheon$+$/DES-5YR/Union3 supernovae, cosmic chronometers, $f\sigma_8$, and compressed Planck
data), SEDE is mildly preferred over $\LCDM$ ($\Delta\mathrm{DIC}\approx-2.9$). A false-preference
calibration indicates this is not obviously attributable to model flexibility ($\sim2$--$3\sigma$,
depending on the CMB-distance treatment), and a pre-DESI~$\to$~DESI split shows a SEDE realisation
trained on pre-DESI data predicts the DESI DR2 BAO trend better than $\LCDM$. The equation of state
crosses $-1$ with $w_a<0$, in DESI's evolving-dark-energy quadrant, and the vacuum-energy-scale problem
is recast via the Cohen--Kaplan--Nelson bound rather than inherited. The quantum-gravitational content
reduces to a single falsifiable postulate---a volume-law (thermalised) horizon entropy---sharply
testable by a measurement of $\Delta$ with DESI DR3 and Euclid (forecast $\sigma(\Delta)\approx0.09$,
$\sim11\sigma$ from a smooth horizon). SEDE is suggestive but not established; the next generation of
surveys will decide.
\end{abstract}
